Exercise 41 — Course Records Table

Modelling exercise — formalising a multi-year database of course records.

The Scenario

At the end of each calendar year a table is produced summarising details of courses that ran during that year. The table has seven columns:

ColumnFieldDescriptionPosition

Coursee.2Three-letter course acronym2
Locatione.3Teaching centre: 478 or 4793
Lecturere.4Lead lecturer4
Attendeese.5Number of attendees5
Submissionse.6Number of assignment submissions (may exceed attendees)6
Averagee.7Grade average7

Part (a) — Axiomatic Definition with Constraints

Task: Complete the axiomatic definition of $records$ with four constraints:

  1. The domain covers years 1993 to the current year — and no others
  2. At most 50 courses per year
  3. Every entry's date belongs to the correct year
  4. No double-booking of teaching rooms on the same date

Step 1 — Understand the task

We need to complete an axiomatic definition. The declaration ($records$ and its type) goes above the line. Below the line, we write four predicates — invariants that must always hold. Each English constraint becomes one formal line.

Step 2 — Identify the approach

We have four English constraints. Each becomes one predicate below the line:

  1. "years 1993 to current, no others" → fix the domain using integer range
  2. "at most 50 courses per year" → ∀ over years, bound #
  3. "entries pertain to that year" → nested ∀ with year extraction
  4. "no double-booking" → ∀ over index pairs with ⇒

Step 3 — Plan the components

Constraint (i): dom records = 1993 .. current. This says "exactly these years, no more, no fewer."

Constraint (ii): ∀ y : dom records • #(records y) ≤ 50.

Constraint (iii): nested ∀ over years y, then over entries e in ran(records y). Check year(e.1) = y.

Constraint (iv): ∀ over tables r in ran records, then over distinct index pairs. Same date ∧ same indices ⇒ different location.

Part (b) — Set Comprehensions

Key pattern: To say "entry $e$ appears somewhere in the records", we write:

$\exists\, r : \text{ran}\ records \bullet e \in \text{ran}\ r$

This says: there exists a table $r$ that is in the range of $records$ (i.e., some year's table) such that $e$ is one of the entries in that table. This pattern appears in sub-parts (i), (ii), and (iii).

(iv) Courses where every instance averages ≥ 70

Task: Write a set comprehension for the course acronyms where every single instance (across all years) achieved an average of at least 70.
